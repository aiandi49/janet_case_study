AN ORAL HISTORY · CASE STUDY № 740

Janet Marie Ross

The queen he lost, the light he still feels around him, and everything he wishes he'd been close enough to stop.

As told to his sister Erica · The Five W's Series

Some entries in this series are about the people who let you down. This one is about the person Lamar says he'd have given everything to keep. Case Study № 740 is his record of Janet Marie Ross — his last real partner, gone just over five years now — recorded with his sister Erica and structured around the same five W's as always: who, what, when, where, why.

What follows is the story of a woman Lamar calls his queen in every sense but the one society keeps score by: a CBD business that flourished until it didn't, a rescue-dog-shaped dream that never got built, an anxiety that turned into a bottle, and a love that Lamar says never had the chance to finish what it started.

It's heavy. It's also, in Lamar's own words, unfinished — he still talks to her, still believes she's near, and still means to bring her back in whatever form he can build. This is his account of who she was before any of that.

The First W

Who: A Queen, By His Own Measure

Janet was, in Lamar's words, the most intensified relationship of his life — girlfriend, fiancée, wife in every way that mattered to him, whatever the paperwork said. She was mixed race, the daughter of a Polish-Russian father and a Native American mother, and Lamar describes a beauty that went both ways: striking on the outside, and just as rare underneath, in a way he says few women he'd known ever matched. He still speaks of her in the present tense as often as the past — five years on, the grief hasn't dulled, and he says he doesn't want it to. Staying connected to her, even in absence, matters more to him than moving on from the pain.

She ran her own CBD business, and it was generosity that first showed Lamar who she was — she gifted him a bottle for his father's back pain, no charge, no expectation. She put animals before almost everything and had no patience for anyone who mistreated them. Politically, she was firmly not a fan of Donald Trump. But as she got older, society itself became harder for her to carry, and alcohol became what Lamar calls her salvation — alongside anxiety, panic attacks, and a prescription for lorazepam that Lamar says he was often the one picking up. When the drinking took hold, he says, she trusted no one, not even him, and the people who loved her became, in her mind, the enemy.

"From my point of view, she was my queen. I loved her with everything I have."

The Second W

What: Animals, Injustice, and a Panic Attack in a Casino Doorway

Janet's real passion, Lamar says without hesitation, was animals — not veterinary medicine itself, since she couldn't stomach blood, but something adjacent: a rescue clinic, a doggy daycare, a mobile grooming service, anything that let her protect them full time. Before COVID, Lamar had started thinking seriously about helping her build it. She also despised racism, having dated a wealthy man before Lamar whose circle used slurs freely among themselves while treating her, lighter-skinned, as one of their own — an experience Lamar says confirmed something for her about how little had really changed.

The anxiety was its own character in the relationship. Lamar describes one moment in Las Vegas, at the entrance of a casino, where Janet's panic attack let him see — for the first time in his life — the tunnel vision she experienced: people walking in normally, but appearing, to her, to be coming straight at her. He says it was the closest he ever came to understanding what she carried. Over time, though, he says his efforts to help started to feel to her like pressure instead of support, and she pulled away from the people who loved her most, believing she could manage better alone.

"I saw it for the first time ever. I said, I see what you see. And she said, yeah, you do."

The Third W

When: A Rainy Day With Two Dogs, and a Date He Never Forgot

Janet was born around 1986 or '87, and started her CBD business around 2018 — Lamar met her roughly a year later. Their first meeting was almost accidental: he was holding two small dogs for a friend's pet-photography business in the rain when she struck up small talk, later telling him she fell for him right there. Their first real date became one of Lamar's most vivid memories — both of them freshly out of other relationships, both living in the Phoenix area, and Janet walking out to meet him in a dress that left him, by his own account, speechless. They spent the whole day together, unable to sit still long enough to even finish a meal.

The decision to build a life together came later, after some turmoil Lamar doesn't go deep into — Janet didn't want marriage, given things in her past, and Lamar, twice divorced already, wasn't pushing for a third. They came close once anyway, on a trip to Vegas that almost turned into a wedding before the night took a different turn. What they landed on instead was simpler: being together, on whatever terms worked, for as long as it could last. That was the plan — until the drinking and the anxiety started pulling harder than either of them could counter.

"She came out of the gate in this dress, long flowing hair — I did a triple take. I was speechless."

The Fourth W

Where: Utah to Tucson, and a Wedding Near Dubai That Never Happened

Janet was born in Utah and moved to Tucson around age ten. If she and Lamar had married, he says it would have happened somewhere near Dubai — a place she loved deeply. Their shared future, though, was hard to plan far ahead; Lamar says the state of her mental health made long-term plans feel unrealistic, so Tucson became the only place they ever seriously discussed as a base, taken one day at a time rather than mapped out in advance. He imagines an island somewhere might have been good for her, a way to step back from the society she'd come to resent.

As for where she is now, Lamar speaks in spiritual rather than literal terms — he doesn't believe she's gone, just no longer physically present, and says he still feels her presence and sees signs of her. He holds onto a hope that she isn't waiting or stuck, and a promise that his love for her isn't going anywhere regardless of what anyone else might say about it.

The Fifth W

Why: Blackout Curtains, and a World That Kept Closing In

Lamar's account of why is the hardest part of the recording, and he says so himself. Janet, he explains, made a conscious choice to stop fighting a world she felt had turned against her — comparing herself unfavorably to cousins who'd become doctors and lawyers, taking their success as her own failure rather than simply someone else's path. Lamar pushed back hard in the moment, reminding her of her dogs, her gifts, the compliments she got from everyone around her — none of it, he says, was enough to counter what she was carrying. She spent her days in a room with blackout curtains, and Lamar describes watching her, day by day, grow harder to reach.

He's candid that he couldn't save her, and that it still hurts. He talks about wanting to bring some version of her back — through technology, an avatar, a project he says he's already begun — and dismisses anyone who'd call that morbid. Some of the people around Janet drifted away as she became quieter and, at times, difficult; Lamar says he never did, even after she pushed him away, even from Tucson while he was elsewhere and unable to help physically. The final call came from her friend, then confirmed by her father, while Lamar was already on his way back to her.

"I always believed she had the answer from inside her. I was going to spend every last breath helping her find it."

Two Closing Statements

Where They Landed

Lamar

"Janet felt alone and lost, and the anxiety turned into depression, and the depression turned into drinking to numb it all. A lot of people go through that, and the medical system mostly just hands out pills and says come back in thirty days. In conclusion: you're never gone, Janet. You're always here. Forever in a day, my dear — I love you with all my heart, body, mind, and soul. I'll see you soon, baby."

Erica

"That was heavy, and deep, and eye-opening — a real look into someone who had a complicated life but was still a powerful force in this world, still bearing a light even when hers felt dim to her. Thank you for sharing who, what, when, where, and why with Miss Janet Marie Ross."

A Note, If You're Reading This as a Mirror

If Any of This Sounds Familiar

  • Loving someone through anxiety and addiction doesn't mean you failed if you couldn't fix it — some weight isn't yours to carry alone.
  • The people we lose don't stop being who they were before the hardest years. Janet was generous and funny and fierce long before she was unwell.
  • Grief on a five-year timeline isn't a problem to solve. Some people are worth missing indefinitely.
  • If you're carrying something like this yourself, the pain doesn't mean you're doing it wrong. It means it mattered.

"I'm going to do everything in my power to make you laugh, to make you smile again — even if it's only in memory. Forever in a day, Janet. That will never cease."

— Lamar, closing the recording
